#+title:  Relicense from AGPL-3.0 to BUSL-1.1 (converting to AGPL after 4 years)
#+author: James Conroy-Finn <james@invetica.co.uk>
#+date:   2026-06-24
#+status: decided

* Context
Bits ships under AGPL-3.0-or-later with a parallel commercial license available
on request. The AGPL's network-use clause forces anyone running a modified Bits
as a service to publish their source. In practice this has not been a strong
deterrent against the failure mode I care about: a well-resourced competitor
taking the codebase, running it as a managed service competitive with the
hosted Bits offering, and competing with Invetica on infrastructure scale
rather than on product or community contribution.

AGPL is a /copyleft/ license. It does not restrict commercial use, hosting, or
direct competition. The "share-alike" obligation is the only friction, and
sophisticated competitors are happy to satisfy it. The protection I actually
want is a /non-compete/ on hosted services — orthogonal to AGPL's design.

The pattern adopted by Sentry, MariaDB, HashiCorp, CockroachDB, and Elastic
addresses this directly: a source-available license with a time-limited
non-compete that converts to a recognised open-source license after a defined
period.

* Decision
Relicense Bits from AGPL-3.0-or-later to the Business Source License 1.1
(BUSL-1.1) with the following parameters:

- *Licensor*: Invetica Ltd
- *Change License*: GNU Affero General Public License v3.0 or later
- *Change Date*: four years after each version's first publicly available
  distribution (per-version, as encoded in BUSL's standard "whichever comes
  first" clause)
- *Additional Use Grant*: production use is permitted except where Bits (or a
  substantial portion of its functionality) is offered to third parties on a
  hosted or embedded basis in competition with Invetica Ltd's paid version(s)
  of Bits

* Why BUSL and not FSL or stock AGPL
** Why not stock AGPL
AGPL gives no protection against hosted competition. The copyleft obligation
is satisfiable and does not restrain a determined competitor. The threat model
is hosted extraction, and AGPL is the wrong instrument for that threat.

** Why not FSL
The Functional Source License (Sentry's current license) is structurally
similar to BUSL but only converts to Apache 2.0 or MIT. Converting from a
source-available license to a permissive license discards copyleft entirely
when the protection window expires. BUSL allows AGPL as the Change License,
which preserves the copyleft endgame: when the four-year window closes on a
given version, that version becomes available under terms that still prevent
proprietary capture downstream.

** Why BUSL
BUSL is the most established source-available license with a configurable
Change License. The legal text is mature, the per-version Change Date is baked
into the standard terms, and SPDX recognises ~BUSL-1.1~ as an identifier so
tooling does not break.

* Parameters in detail
** Change Date — four years per version
BUSL's standard terms state that the license effectively converts on "the
Change Date, or the fourth anniversary of the first publicly available
distribution of a specific version of the Licensed Work under this License,
whichever comes first." This makes the per-version four-year cap automatic.

The =LICENSE= file on =main= uses relative phrasing ("four years from the date
this version is first publicly distributed") to mirror this. No release tooling
needs to substitute a concrete date — the per-version semantics are encoded in
the license text itself.

** Additional Use Grant — non-compete SaaS
Production use is explicitly permitted for everyone. The grant only restricts
offering Bits as a hosted or embedded service in competition with Invetica
Ltd's paid offering. Self-hosting, internal use, modifications, contributions,
forks for non-competing use, and audits remain unrestricted.

This intentionally matches the Sentry / FSL non-compete wording, which has
been tested in the wild and is well-understood by both contributors and
counsel.

** Change License — AGPL-3.0-or-later
On the Change Date for a given version, that version becomes available under
AGPL-3.0-or-later. The copyleft endgame is preserved: anyone running an
AGPL-converted version as a service must publish their source.

* Implications
** Pre-relicense releases stay AGPL
Anyone who pulled Bits from a commit prior to the relicensing commit holds
AGPL-3.0-or-later rights to that snapshot in perpetuity. The new license only
binds people who take code after the switch. This is a property of how
licensing works — you cannot retroactively revoke rights you have already
granted.

** Loss of OSI / FSF status
BUSL is not an OSI-approved open-source license and is not a free-software
license under the FSF's definition. The repository and marketing material
should use "source-available" rather than "open source" when describing Bits.

** Commercial license value
The existing commercial license offer continues to apply. Under BUSL it lifts
two distinct restrictions: (1) the AGPL-style copyleft that takes effect on
the Change Date, and (2) the SaaS non-compete that applies before the Change
Date. The commercial pitch sharpens because the SaaS-competition case is now
the canonical reason to buy.
